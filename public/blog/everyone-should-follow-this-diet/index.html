Everyone Should Follow [Insert Diet Here]

**The reality:** There's no one-size-fits-all diet. What works for your friend, your trainer, or the influencer you follow might be terrible for YOUR body.

Myth #3: “Everyone Should Follow [Insert Diet Here]”

Keto. Paleo. Vegan. Carnivore. Low-fat. High-carb. Intermittent fasting.

The claim: There’s ONE perfect diet that works for everyone.

The reality: There’s no one-size-fits-all diet. What works for your friend, your trainer, or the influencer you follow might be terrible for YOUR body.

Why This Myth Is Harmful:

1. It Ignores Bio-Individuality

Your genetics, metabolic type, gut microbiome, stress levels, activity level, and health history all affect how you respond to food.

Example: Keto

  • Person A: Thrives on keto (high fat, low carb). Energy soars, mental clarity improves, weight drops.
  • Person B: Feels terrible on keto. Exhausted, brain fog, workouts suffer, thyroid tanks.

Why? They have different metabolic types.

  • Person A is a fast oxidizer (burns through carbs quickly, needs more fat and protein)
  • Person B is a slow oxidizer (needs more carbs for energy, too much fat makes them sluggish)

The diet isn’t “good” or “bad”—it’s right or wrong for YOUR body.

2. It Creates Orthorexia and Food Fear

When you believe there’s ONE right way to eat, you develop:

  • Fear of “bad” foods
  • Guilt when you eat outside the diet
  • Social isolation (can’t eat at restaurants, avoid gatherings)
  • Obsession with food rules

Health isn’t about perfection. It’s about finding what works for your body and sustainably supporting it.

3. It Ignores Food Sensitivities

You can eat a “perfect” diet—organic, whole foods, no processed junk—and still feel terrible if you’re eating foods YOUR body reacts to.

Common “healthy” foods that cause reactions:

  • Eggs (high in histamine, common IgG sensitivity)
  • Almonds (oxalates, IgG sensitivity)
  • Spinach (high oxalates—can contribute to kidney stones, joint pain)
  • Tomatoes (nightshade—triggers inflammation for some)
  • Gluten-free grains (cross-reactivity in Celiac patients)

You won’t know unless you test.

What to Do Instead:

1. Determine Your Metabolic Type

  • Metabolic Typing questionnaire or blood test
  • Identifies if you’re a fast oxidizer, slow oxidizer, or mixed type
  • Tells you your ideal macronutrient ratio (protein/fat/carb balance)

2. Test for Food Sensitivities

  • IgG food sensitivity panel (tests 150+ foods)
  • Identifies delayed reactions (not the same as allergies)
  • Remove reactive foods for 90 days, heal the gut, reintroduce

3. Follow Universal Principles, Then Personalize

Universal principles (work for almost everyone):

  • Eat whole, unprocessed foods
  • Get adequate protein (0.7-1g per pound of body weight)
  • Include healthy fats (avocado, olive oil, nuts, seeds, fatty fish)
  • Eat plenty of vegetables (fiber, nutrients, antioxidants)
  • Balance blood sugar (don’t skip meals, avoid high-carb meals without protein/fat)
  • Stay hydrated

Then personalize:

  • Adjust carb/fat ratio based on metabolic type
  • Remove foods YOU react to
  • Time meals based on YOUR energy and digestion
  • Adjust based on activity level, stress, sleep

Bottom Line: Stop chasing the “perfect diet.” Start eating for YOUR body based on objective data (metabolic typing + food sensitivities).
